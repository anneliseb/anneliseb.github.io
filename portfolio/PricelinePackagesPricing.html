In January 2019, I joined the packages team. The packages team’s goal is to allow the customer to search and book multiple products at once for their trip, while getting a great deal and Priceline’s lowest prices. The existing team had already launched a usability test on usertesting.com, to observe users interactions with a new experience called Trip Builder.

{01} Empathize

The first step was to observe the participants of the usability testing (mentioned above). The product manager and I independently watched the videos and took notes on our observations.

{02} Define

After observing particpants use the product, the product manager I was partnering with and I gathered After observing participants use the product, the product manager I was partnering with and I gathered together our findings and observstions. I lead the PM through a whiteboard exercise, grouping our findings into themes. We identified 3 key areas that caused our users problems that we wanted to focus on. One of those areas was how we display pricing to our customer.

The problem: At different points in the funnel, users struggled to understand what the price shown represented or included. This often caused the user to think products they were searching for were more expensive then they actually were.

{03} Ideate

I created different pricing display models and narrowed it down to four options. I also discovered that how a customers percieves pricing can change depending on their entrypoint. I therefore broke the pricing displays into two entry point categories: starting from scratch vs. starting with a base trip that has a $ amount associated with it.

{04} Prototype

Based off of the fact that I had many ideas and was hoping to narrow them down to one or two to explore further, I went with paper prototyping as my methodology. It was very important in this protoype to make sure the content (especially the prices) made sense to the customer. Cue the PM I work with! The PM helped me with the prototype by creating a spreadsheet for each pricing display model which was accurate to the product choices a customer might make in the prototype.

It’s also worth mentioning that one of the pricing display models Priceline is not actually capable of today. I felt it was important to include as an idea, because if it did well the team could consider the work needed in the API to allow that display.

{05} Test

The results from the paper prototyping were that utilizing incremental pricing display and component pricing were preferred. Component pricing was the option Priceline isn’t capable of yet today.

I also learned that while showing two entry points allowed for more pricing displays, it skewed the results slightly because particpants had strong views on the differences between those entry points.

With that said, I still felt confident that incremental pricing was the way to go and the team made the changes and launched the Total + Incremental pricing display A/B test in production.

{06} A/B Test and Next Steps

The A/B test further proved that the findings from the paper prototyping were correct. The test had a win probability of 97% and showed an improvement of 3.03% over the default experience.
